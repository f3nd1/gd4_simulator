# Risk and Remediation — Prioritising What to Fix First

Not every gap carries the same risk. When an institution has multiple findings, a good advisor helps them sequence remediation so the highest-stakes items are fixed before the lower-stakes ones.

## Risk-stratify findings by regulatory and certification consequence

**Category A — Regulatory breach (fix immediately, before re-audit)**
These gaps are not just scoring weaknesses; they are violations of CPE mandatory requirements. A CPE assessor finding them may trigger enforcement action separate from the EduTrust band outcome.
- Student contracts not signed before fee collection (GD4 4.2.1)
- Fees collected without Fee Protection Scheme coverage (GD4 4.2.2)
- International students enrolled without a valid EduTrust certification
- Refund not processed per the CPE mandatory refund table
- Pre-course counselling not conducted or not recorded

**Category B — Star-disqualifying gaps**
These don't violate regulations but will prevent a 4-Year (Star) rating regardless of how strong other criteria are.
- Criterion 7 (outcomes) band below 3 — the 330-point weight means a weak Criterion 7 cannot be compensated elsewhere
- Any criterion with a Band 1 — a single Band 1 creates a floor that prevents Star even if all others are Band 4-5
- Academic Board governance gaps — CPE assessors scrutinise Academic Board independence and substantive involvement closely for Star

**Category C — Band-limiting gaps**
These cap the overall score but are not regulatory or certification blockers. Prioritise after A and B.
- Missing review records on otherwise well-implemented processes
- Outcome data collected but not analysed
- Records incomplete (present for most but not all)

**Category D — Enhancement opportunities**
These are the investments that lift from Band 3 to Band 4 or Band 4 to Band 5. Do these after Categories A-C are resolved.
- Building external benchmarking into outcome reporting
- Strengthening Academic Board minutes to show substantive decisions
- Documenting staff handover and system resilience

## The 90-day remediation window (typical pre-audit)

If an institution has 90 days before their EduTrust submission, a realistic sequence:

**Days 1–30:** Address all Category A items. These require actual evidence of events (contracts signed, FPS certificates issued, counselling conducted), so they cannot be created quickly — but outstanding ones can be completed for current students and retrospective gaps documented and explained.

**Days 31–60:** Address Category B gaps, focusing on Criterion 7 data collection and any Band 1 items. Create the outcome analysis that is missing. Run the Academic Board meeting that should have happened. Document it properly.

**Days 61–90:** Category C gap closure — complete the missing records, conduct the reviews that were skipped, write the management responses to audit findings.

## Red-line statements to make early

If you identify a Category A gap in your assessment, state it clearly and first:

- "This is a regulatory compliance requirement, not just an EduTrust scoring gap. It must be rectified before the assessment — CPE may treat it as a separate enforcement matter."
- "Fixing this after the audit notification has been received is too late for it to count as proactive compliance."

If Criterion 7 is weak: "Criterion 7 carries 330 of 1,000 points. A Band 2 Criterion 7 makes a 4-Year (Star) outcome arithmetically impossible regardless of scores elsewhere."

## Common remediation mistakes to warn against

1. **Policy refresh without records.** Rewriting all the policies in the 60 days before audit without generating matching implementation records produces a Band 2 pattern (good approach, no processes evidence).
2. **Retroactively completing forms.** Completing blank appraisal or counselling forms for past periods. This is document fabrication — warn the institution that assessors are trained to detect this and it is a serious integrity risk.
3. **Meeting minutes without meetings.** Writing detailed-looking minutes for meetings that did not happen. Same issue as above.
4. **Fixing the sample, not the system.** Generating records for the specific students or dates that appear in the gap list, without addressing the underlying system failure. The assessor will re-sample — and this approach makes the institution look worse, not better.
