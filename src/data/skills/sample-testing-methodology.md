# Sample Testing Methodology — Audit Sampling Standards

An auditor who reviews all records finds no time to audit anything else. An auditor who reviews two records has no basis for a conclusion. Apply these sampling standards to frame observations correctly and to call out when a claimed conclusion has insufficient coverage.

## Minimum sample sizes for GD4 audit testing

| Population size | Minimum sample | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1–10 records | 100% | Review all |
| 11–30 records | 5–8 (≥50%) | Name which records and why selected |
| 31–100 records | 10–15 (≥15%) | Use random or systematic selection |
| 101–500 records | 20–25 | Risk-stratify: oversample recent intake and highest-risk cohort |
| 500+ records | 30–40 | Include representation from each cohort/intake |

**Express findings as rates, not counts**: "7 of 23 files reviewed (30%)" — always state both numerator and denominator. A bare "7 files were missing" with no denominator is not a defensible finding.

## Selection strategy

- **Random sample**: Assign numbers, use a random sequence. Avoids selection bias.
- **Risk-stratified sample**: Oversample higher-risk strata (international students for FPS, new intake for counselling, near-threshold students for attendance). State the stratification rationale.
- **Systematic sample**: Every Nth record. Simple and audit-defensible.
- **Judgement sample** (use sparingly): Deliberately select records where a gap is suspected. Valid for targeted testing, but state that results may not be representative of the whole population.

## What to state in an observation when reporting a sample

"The auditor selected [N] [record type] from a population of [M] using [random / systematic / risk-stratified] sampling, covering [period]. [X] of [N] sampled records showed [gap]. Extrapolating to the full population (noting the sample limitation), this suggests [estimated impact or range]."

## Calling out insufficient coverage in evidence

If the institution provides only 1–3 examples as evidence for a requirement covering hundreds of students or multiple years, name that as insufficient:
"The institution provided 2 sample counselling forms as evidence. With an intake of [N] students, a sample of 2 (< 1%) is not sufficient to conclude that the process was applied consistently across the cohort. A minimum of [recommended N] files should be reviewed."

## Response rate sufficiency for surveys/outcome data

Outcome statistics drawn from surveys must state the response rate. Apply the thresholds below:
- **≥ 70% response rate** → results are representative; can be cited as an institutional outcome metric.
- **40–69%** → results are indicative; note the response rate limitation in the finding and flag as partial evidence.
- **< 40%** → results are not representative; state in findings as "unsubstantiated by survey methodology" — the claim cannot support a Systems & Outcomes rating above "Limited."

Employment rate claims are particularly common targets: always demand the survey instrument, the population (all graduates or only respondents?), the response rate, and the methodology (self-reported vs verified). A 95% employment rate on a 15% response rate is a finding, not a positive outcome.

## Extrapolation language

Use hedged language when a sample does not cover the full population:
- "Based on the [N]-record sample, the auditor estimates that approximately [X%] of the population may be affected. A full population review is recommended before the EduTrust assessment."
- Never extrapolate from fewer than 5 records or write "all records" when only a sample was reviewed.

## Document the sample in the finding

Every finding based on a sample must include: how many records were in scope, how many were reviewed, how they were selected, and what the gap rate was in the sample. This is what makes the finding defensible to an SSG assessor.
