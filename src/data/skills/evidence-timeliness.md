# Evidence Timeliness — Auditing Dates, Coverage Periods, and Suspicious Patterns

A document that exists is not the same as a document that was in place during the review period. Date-audit every piece of evidence: creation date, version date, approval date, and the period it covers. These distinctions separate genuine compliance from audit-preparation compliance.

## The review period

GD4 EduTrust assessments cover the **certification cycle** (typically 4 years, or the Provisional period). Evidence must span the full cycle, not just the most recent semester — **for recurring and high-volume processes** (attendance, fee collection, contracts, feedback logs, management/academic review, appraisals). For inherently low-frequency or event-driven activities (a course transfer, a withdrawal, a marketing vetting, an appeal), "full cycle" means the evidence covers the events that actually occurred — a genuine record per event, not a record in every month. Do not treat a small number of event-driven records as a timeliness gap when few such events happened. A policy dated 3 months before the audit that purports to have governed the institution for 4 years is a timeliness finding.

## Red flags — document dating

1. **Created within 4 weeks of the audit submission deadline**: Note it as "the document appears to have been prepared in anticipation of the audit rather than governing practice during the review period."
2. **Version date post-dates the events it governs**: A 2025 refund policy that applies to a 2023 student withdrawal — was the correct policy in place in 2023? If no prior version is sighted, note the gap.
3. **Meeting minutes clustered at the audit date**: A Management Review record dated one week before submission, with no prior annual reviews — this is audit-preparation, not a functioning review cycle.
4. **Records with uniform, sequential dates**: An attendance register where all entries have the same handwriting and were clearly written at one time, or where dates are in implausibly perfect sequence, suggests retrospective completion.
5. **Evidence period shorter than the review period**: Attendance records covering only 3 months of a 12-month review period. Policies approved in late 2024 with no evidence they governed practice in 2022–2024.

## What to check per evidence type

**Policies and procedures:**
- Version date vs earliest event the policy should govern.
- Approval date and approving body.
- Is the version date consistent with the document's own "reviewed by" section?

**Meeting minutes:**
- Date, attendees, agenda items — verify the agenda matches the claimed discussion.
- Gap between meeting dates — annual review meetings should be approximately 12 months apart. Meetings 18–24 months apart suggest the review cycle is not functioning.
- Minutes produced after the fact? Check: are action items in the minutes consistent with what actually happened, or are they retrospectively recorded decisions?

**Training records / attendance logs / registers:**
- Do records span the full review period or only a recent window?
- Are there unexplained gaps in the record (e.g. no attendance data for a semester)?
- Electronic records: check for consistent font, formatting, or metadata that suggests bulk editing.

**Contracts and agreements:**
- Execution date vs the date the relationship actually began. An agency agreement dated 6 months after the agent first sent students is a control gap.
- Student contract date vs enrolment/fee-collection date.

**Survey results / outcome data:**
- Year of the survey vs the claimed period of the outcome.
- Trend data: is the same year's data being re-used across multiple reports?

## Language for timeliness findings

- "The [document name] is dated [date], which is [X days/weeks] before the audit submission deadline. The auditor was unable to verify that this document was in place and governing practice during the [full review period / specific earlier period]."
- "Meeting minutes were provided for [date only]. No further management review records were sighted for the preceding [N months/years], suggesting the review cycle is not functioning on the required [annual/quarterly] basis."
- "The [register/log] covers [period provided]. The full review period is [cycle dates]. A gap of [N months] in the record is unexplained and may indicate the process was not being applied during that interval."
- "The student contract execution date ([date]) post-dates the first fee payment ([date]) by [N days], indicating that the contract was signed after fees were collected — a regulatory breach under GD4 4.2."

## Cycle coverage — what "full cycle" means

For a 4-Year certification: evidence must be traceable across all four years, not just the most recent. For outcome data (Criterion 7), at least 2–3 complete cycles (intakes or academic years) are needed to demonstrate trend. Single-year data is Descriptive (Band 2–3); multi-year trend with target comparison is Analytical (Band 4).
